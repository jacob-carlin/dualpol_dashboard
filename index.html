Notes & Disclaimer

This dashboard is an implementation of the polarimetric radar forward operator described in Ryzhkov et al. (2011) and is designed to help illustrate the behavior of the polarimetric radar variables as a function of various tunable radar and microphysical parameters.

Microphysical Treatments and Assumptions:

Dielectric constants:

  • The temperature-dependent complex dielectric constant of water is calculated according to Ray (1972).
  • The complex dielectric constant of ice and air are assumed to be invariant to temperature and wavelength and fixed at values of 3.18+0.001j and 1.0006+0.0000005j, respectively.
  • For snow and graupel with densities ≤ solid ice, two mixing formula options are provided: Maxwell Garnett (1904) and Polder and van Santen (1946). For both mixing formula options, the dielectric constant of dry snow is found according to Eq. (4) of Ryzhkov et al. (2011). The Maxwell Garnett (1904) mixing formula further requires the choice of which media (water vs. snow) is considered the background versus inclusions; the Polder and van Santen (1946) formula is symmetric and does not require this delineation.
  • Hail is assumed to have a density of solid ice (917 kg m-3), with meltwater accumulating on its surface. The temperature of the water shell is assumed to be 0°C.

Orientations:

  • For all particles, the mean canting angle is assumed to be 0° (i.e., no preferred orientation). The adjustable canting angle distribution width refers to the standard deviation of the distribution following Eq. (20) of Ryzhkov et al. (2011).

Shapes:

  • All particles are assumed to be oblate spheroids with a maximum axis ratio of 1.0 (i.e., spherical).
  • For rain, the axis ratio is constrained and found as a function of particle equivolume diameter following Brandes et al. (2005). The axis ratios of melting snow/graupel and dry hail are adjustable.
  • For melting hail, a meltwater torus is approximated using Eq. (32) of Carlin and Ryzhkov (2025), which adapts the meltwater model of Kumjian et al. (2018) to meltwater values less than the critical threshold. This model evenly distributes the liquid water around the hailstone whose shape is supplied by the user. If the meltwater thickness is ≤ 0.5 mm, the water remains evenly distributed. Otherwise, the meltwater thickness at the top and bottom of the particle are fixed at 0.5 mm and is allowed to increase on the particle’s equator down to a minimum axis ratio of 0.56.

Scattering:

  • For simplicity, Rayleigh scattering is assumed for all particles; a warning will pop up to alert users when this condition is being violated (defined by the Resonance Parameter (RP) exceeding 0.6).
  • For rain and snow/graupel, the Rayleigh forward- and back-scattering amplitudes are calculated according to Eq. (22) of Ryzhkov et al. (2011).
  • For hail, the two-layer spheroid model of Bohren and Huffman (1983) following Eq. (28) of Ryzhkov et al. (2011).
  • Final radar variables are calculated following Eq. (29) of Ryzhkov et al. (2011) and assuming N(D)dD = 1.
